A practical guide to using AI as your writing buddy — with human oversight

The big picture

ℹ️ GenAI is a buddy, not a replacement. You stay in charge of the thinking, the argument, and the final text. AI helps you get unstuck, explore ideas, and polish your work — but you own the output.
🧠 GenAI in the writing process

AI can assist at every stage of academic writing — prewriting, drafting, revising, and editing. The key is human agency: you direct the process, evaluate the output, and make the final decisions.

At each stage, AI plays a different support role. In prewriting, it helps brainstorm. In drafting, it suggests vocabulary and transitions. In revising, it checks logical flow. In editing, it catches grammar errors. But the critical thinking stays with you.

🎯 Prompting techniques

A prompt is the instruction you give AI. Better prompts produce better results — this is like giving clear directions to someone. There are five key techniques: zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought, role prompting, and iterative prompting.

Mastering these techniques lets you optimize results through skillful interaction rather than accepting whatever the AI first produces.

⚠️

Human Agency Is Non-Negotiable

AI can accelerate your writing, but it cannot think for you. Always evaluate AI output critically. Don't offload thinking during prewriting. Ask AI to explain errors rather than just fixing them. Your name goes on the work — make sure it reflects your understanding.

Key terms

📘 Glossary

GenAI (Generative AI) — AI systems that produce text, images, or other content based on patterns learned from data.

Prompt — The instruction or question you give to an AI model.

Zero-shot — Giving a direct instruction with no examples.

Few-shot — Providing examples alongside your instruction to guide the format or style.

Chain-of-thought — Asking the AI to reason step by step.

Role prompting — Assigning a persona (e.g., "Act as an academic editor").

Iterative prompting — Refining output across multiple exchanges in a conversation.

Human agency — The writer's responsibility to direct, evaluate, and own the final output.

GenAI can support each stage of the writing process — but always with human oversight. Below is how AI fits into each phase, with practical examples and cautions.

The four stages

Stage 1 🧠 Prewriting

Brainstorm ideas, create outlines, and explore concepts. AI can help generate topic angles, suggest structures, and explain unfamiliar concepts.

⚠️ Caution: Don't offload your thinking. Use AI to spark ideas, not to replace your own critical analysis.

Example prompts

Brainstorm five possible angles for an essay on urban migration in Finland.
Outline a structure for a 2,000-word literature review on renewable energy policy.
Stage 2 ✍️ Drafting

Overcome writer's block, find better vocabulary, and propose transitions between paragraphs. AI can suggest phrasing, but the argument and structure should be yours.

Example prompts

Suggest three academic transition phrases to connect a paragraph about causes to one about effects.
Rephrase this sentence in a more formal academic register: "People really don't like climate change."
Stage 3 🔍 Revising

Check logical flow, clarify arguments, and adjust tone. AI can read your draft and point out structural weaknesses or unclear reasoning.

Example prompts

Review this paragraph for logical consistency and suggest improvements.
Identify the weakest argument in my essay and explain why it's weak.
Stage 4 📝 Editing & Proofreading

Spot grammar errors, ensure consistent formatting, and check citation style. This is where AI is most straightforward — but always ask it to explain the errors so you learn.

💡 Tip: Ask AI to explain errors, not just fix them. "Why is this sentence incorrect?" teaches you more than "Fix this."

Example prompts

Proofread this paragraph and explain each grammar error you find.
Check whether my in-text citations follow APA 7th edition format.
🧑‍💻

Human Agency Runs Through Every Stage

The cycle is not linear — you may loop between revising and drafting, or return to prewriting after a failed draft. At every point, you decide what to keep, what to cut, and what to rethink. AI supports; you lead.

Think of prompting as giving directions. The clearer and more specific your instructions, the better the result. Here are five techniques, from simplest to most advanced.

The five techniques

🎯 1. Zero-Shot

Give a direct instruction with no examples. Best for simple, clear-cut tasks.

"List three effects of urbanisation on mental health."

📋 2. Few-Shot

Provide examples alongside your instruction to guide the AI's style, format, or reasoning pattern.

"Summarise the following articles in the style of these two examples: [Example 1] [Example 2]."

⚙️ 3. Chain-of-Thought

Ask the AI to reason step by step. This improves accuracy for complex or multi-step tasks.

"Analyse this argument step by step. First identify the claim, then the evidence, then evaluate the reasoning."

🎭 4. Role Prompting

Assign a persona to the AI. This shapes the tone, vocabulary, and perspective of the response.

"Act as an academic editor. Review my introduction for clarity and coherence."

🔄 5. Iterative Prompting

Refine output through conversation. Don't accept the first response — follow up, narrow down, and improve.

"Good start, but make it more concise and add a counter-argument in the second paragraph."

How they connect

ℹ️ These techniques are not mutually exclusive. A strong prompt often combines several: you might use role prompting + few-shot + chain-of-thought in a single instruction. Iterative prompting is always layered on top.
🔗 Combined example

Prompt

Act as an academic writing tutor (role). Analyse the following paragraph step by step (chain-of-thought). Use the format shown in this example (few-shot): [Example]. Focus on argument structure and coherence.

Then, based on the AI's response, you might follow up (iterative): "Now focus specifically on the transition between paragraphs 2 and 3."
