ℹ️ AI tools are powerful, but they are not neutral. They reflect the data they were trained on, the choices their designers made, and the infrastructure that powers them. Using AI responsibly means understanding what can go wrong and what principles should guide use.

Key terms

📘 Glossary

Hallucination — When an AI generates false information presented with high confidence, as though it were fact. The model is not "lying" — it is producing statistically plausible text that happens to be wrong.

Bias (in AI) — Systematic errors or discrimination in AI output caused by biased training data or design choices. Often described as "garbage in, garbage out."

Digital pollution — The environmental cost of training and running AI systems: energy consumption, water use for cooling, and carbon emissions from data centres.

Copyright (in AI context) — The legal and ethical issue of AI models trained on copyrighted works without the creators' permission.

Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics — A set of fictional rules from Isaac Asimov's science fiction, often used as a thought experiment for AI ethics. They are not a practical framework.

UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI — The first global standard on AI ethics, adopted by 193 member states in November 2021. It emphasises transparency, fairness, human oversight, and accountability.

Human oversight — The principle that humans should remain in control of AI systems, especially in high-stakes decisions affecting people's rights and well-being.

🧠❓ Hallucinations

AI generates false information with high confidence. Language models predict the most likely next word rather than verifying facts, so they can produce text that reads convincingly but is entirely fabricated — including fake citations, invented statistics, and non-existent sources.

Example: Ask an AI for academic references on a niche topic. It may return author names, journal titles, and DOIs that look real but do not exist.

⚖️ Bias

AI amplifies discrimination from biased data. If the training data reflects historical inequalities — gender, racial, cultural — the model will reproduce and sometimes intensify those patterns. This is often summarised as "garbage in, garbage out."

Example: A hiring AI trained on historical data may penalise CVs from women if past hiring patterns favoured men.

🌍⚡ Environmental Impact

Training large AI models is energy-intensive and contributes to "digital pollution." A single training run for a large language model can emit as much carbon as several transatlantic flights. Data centres also consume significant water for cooling.

Perspective: Training GPT-3 reportedly consumed approximately 1,287 MWh of energy and emitted around 552 tonnes of CO₂.

Copyright & Privacy

©️🔒 Copyright & Privacy

AI models are trained on works used without permission and may process insecure user data. Writers, artists, and programmers have found their work reproduced without consent or compensation. User inputs may also be stored and used for further training.

Example: Multiple lawsuits (e.g., The New York Times v. OpenAI, 2023) allege that AI training data included copyrighted material without licence.

Why these risks matter for academic work

🎓 Implications for students and researchers

Hallucinations mean you cannot trust AI-generated references or facts without independent verification. Always check sources.

Bias means AI output may reflect dominant perspectives and marginalise minority viewpoints. Be especially cautious with AI-generated content on topics involving culture, gender, race, or social issues.

Environmental impact raises questions about the proportionality of AI use — is using a massive model justified for a simple task?

Copyright and privacy mean you should never input confidential data (personal information, unpublished research, proprietary business data) into public AI tools without understanding the platform's data policy.

⚠️ Rule of thumb: Treat AI output like advice from a confident but unreliable colleague. It may be useful, but it always needs to be checked.

Ethical frameworks help us move from identifying risks to taking principled action. Here we examine two contrasting approaches: a famous thought experiment from fiction and a practical international standard adopted by 193 countries.

Ethical approaches

🤖 Thought Experiment — Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics

Theoretical — not practical

Isaac Asimov introduced these rules in his 1942 short story "Runaround" as a fictional framework for governing robot behaviour. They are often referenced in AI ethics discussions, but they were designed as a narrative device, not a real-world policy tool.

  1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
  2. A robot must obey the orders given it by human beings, except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.
  3. A robot must protect its own existence, as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.
💡 Why they fail in practice: Asimov's own stories repeatedly show these laws producing contradictions and unintended consequences. Real AI systems lack the contextual understanding needed to interpret abstract principles like "harm." The Laws are valuable as a thinking exercise, not as a governance model.
🌐 Practical Guidance — UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI (2021)

Human-rights approach

Adopted in November 2021 by all 193 UNESCO member states, this is the first global standard on AI ethics. Unlike Asimov's fictional rules, it provides concrete policy actions and implementation tools for governments, organisations, and educators.

The Recommendation is built on four core values:

Human Rights & Dignity Peaceful & Just Societies Diversity & Inclusiveness Environment & Ecosystems

These values translate into key operational principles:

Transparency Fairness Human Oversight Accountability Do No Harm
