The big picture

ℹ️ AI is not one thing — it is an umbrella term covering many different technologies, from simple spam filters to large language models. Understanding the categories and limitations is essential for responsible use in academic and professional contexts.
🕰️ History — Why it matters

The idea of "thinking machines" is over a century old. The term "Artificial Intelligence" was coined in 1956, but the cultural and scientific roots go back further — to Karel Čapek's 1920 play and Alan Turing's 1950 thought experiment. Understanding this timeline helps you see that today's GenAI tools are part of a long evolution, not an overnight revolution.

🧠 Concepts — How AI is organised

AI divides broadly into Narrow (Weak) AI — systems designed for specific tasks like spam filtering or voice assistants — and the hypothetical General (Strong) AI, which would match human-level reasoning across all domains. Generative AI (GenAI), such as ChatGPT, is a subset of Narrow AI: it produces text, images, or code based on pattern prediction, not understanding.

🎨 Metaphors — Making AI understandable

Because AI is complex, we rely on metaphors to explain it. Three common ones are: the Black Box (we see input and output but not the internal process), the Iceberg (the visible interface hides massive data processing), and the Stochastic Parrot (the model mimics language patterns without genuine understanding). Each metaphor highlights a different limitation.

Key terms

📘 Glossary

Artificial Intelligence (AI) — The broad field of computer science focused on creating systems that can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence.

Narrow / Weak AI — AI designed for one specific task (e.g., Siri, spam filters, chess engines).

General / Strong AI (AGI) — A hypothetical AI that could perform any intellectual task a human can. This does not yet exist.

Generative AI (GenAI) — AI systems that generate new content (text, images, code) by predicting patterns in training data. Examples: ChatGPT, DALL-E.

Turing Test — A test proposed by Alan Turing in 1950: if a machine can fool a human into thinking it is also human in conversation, it can be said to exhibit intelligent behaviour.

Stochastic Parrot — A metaphor from a 2021 paper by Bender et al., describing large language models as systems that produce fluent-sounding text without genuine comprehension.

Black Box — A metaphor for AI systems whose internal decision-making process is opaque or unexplainable.

Interactive timeline

1920
Karel Čapek coins "Robot"
Czech playwright Karel Čapek introduced the word "robot" (from the Czech robota, meaning forced labour) in his play R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots). The play imagined artificial workers, laying the cultural groundwork for discussions about artificial beings and machine labour.
1950
Alan Turing's "Imitation Game"
In his landmark paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," Alan Turing proposed the Imitation Game (now called the Turing Test) as a way to evaluate whether a machine can exhibit intelligent behaviour indistinguishable from a human. He asked: "Can machines think?" — a question that still frames AI research today.
1956
Dartmouth Conference — "AI" is born
The Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence, organised by John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon, officially coined the term "Artificial Intelligence." This workshop is widely considered the founding moment of AI as a field of study. The proposal boldly stated that "every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it."
1966
ELIZA — the first chatbot
Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT created ELIZA, a program that used simple pattern matching to simulate a Rogerian psychotherapist. Users often attributed human-like understanding to ELIZA despite its rudimentary design — an early example of the "ELIZA effect," where people project intelligence onto machines.
1997
Deep Blue beats Kasparov
IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov — a landmark for Narrow AI. Deep Blue could evaluate 200 million positions per second, but it had no understanding of chess strategy in a human sense. It was a brute-force specialist, not a general thinker.
2011
Siri & the age of virtual assistants
Apple launched Siri, bringing Narrow AI into millions of pockets. Siri uses natural language processing to interpret voice commands and perform specific tasks — setting alarms, sending messages, searching the web. It popularised the idea of AI as a daily tool, though its capabilities remain firmly in the Narrow AI category.
2017
The Transformer architecture
Google researchers published "Attention Is All You Need," introducing the Transformer architecture. This innovation — based on a mechanism called self-attention — made it possible to train much larger language models more efficiently. Transformers underpin virtually all modern GenAI models including GPT, Claude, and Gemini.
2022–Present
Rise of Generative AI
The release of ChatGPT in November 2022 brought GenAI into mainstream awareness. These tools — built on large language models — generate text, images, and code by predicting the most likely next token based on patterns in their training data. They are powerful Narrow AI tools, but they do not "understand" content the way humans do. This distinction is critical for responsible academic use.
💡 Notice how the timeline moves from imagination (fiction, thought experiments) to formalisation (academic research) to application (consumer products). AI's development mirrors this pattern repeatedly.

Categories explained

🔧 Narrow / Weak AI

Designed and trained for one specific task. It excels within its domain but cannot transfer knowledge to other tasks.

Examples: Siri, spam filters, chess engines, recommendation algorithms, image recognition

🧪 General / Strong AI (AGI)

A hypothetical system with human-level reasoning across all domains — learning, planning, and problem-solving in any context. This does not currently exist.

Status: Theoretical — no working example exists as of 2025

⚡ Generative AI (GenAI)

A subset of Narrow AI that generates new content — text, images, code, audio — by predicting patterns in training data. It does not "understand" in the human sense.

Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, DALL-E, Midjourney, GitHub Copilot

📊 Key distinction

GenAI tools feel intelligent because they produce fluent output, but they operate through statistical pattern matching, not comprehension. Recognising this gap is fundamental to using these tools critically and responsibly.

Pattern prediction ≠ understanding
⚠️ Common misconception: "ChatGPT is Strong AI." It is not. ChatGPT and similar tools are sophisticated Narrow AI. They are trained on specific data to perform specific tasks (generating text). They cannot reason about novel problems outside their training distribution.

Metaphors help us explain complex AI systems to non-specialist audiences. Each one highlights a different limitation or characteristic. Understanding these metaphors makes you a more critical user of AI tools.

Three key metaphors

📦
Lack of transparency

The Black Box

You put data in, you get results out — but the process in between is opaque. Many AI models, especially deep neural networks, make decisions that even their creators cannot fully explain. This raises questions about accountability and trust, particularly in high-stakes domains like medicine, law, and hiring.

💡 Why it matters: If you cannot explain how an AI reached a conclusion, how can you evaluate whether it is right?
🧊
Hidden complexity

The Iceberg

The user interface — the chat window, the prompt box — is just the tip. Below the surface lies an enormous infrastructure: billions of parameters, terabytes of training data, massive compute clusters, and complex preprocessing pipelines. What looks simple on the surface is the product of immense engineering and data.

💡 Why it matters: Simple-looking tools have massive environmental, economic, and ethical footprints hidden beneath the interface.
🦜
Mimicry without understanding

The Stochastic Parrot

Coined by Emily Bender, Timnit Gebru, and colleagues in a 2021 paper, this metaphor describes large language models as systems that produce fluent text by stitching together statistical patterns — like a parrot that can mimic speech without understanding it. The output sounds meaningful but is generated without any genuine comprehension of the content.

⚠️ Critical implication: Fluency is not a reliable signal of accuracy. Always verify AI-generated claims against credible sources.
